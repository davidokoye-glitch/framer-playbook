Skill Gap Disclosure — Read Before Part 1

The three non-negotiable prerequisites and who this playbook is built for

🛑
Blunt Truth
If you follow this playbook with significant gaps in the three skill areas below, you will build something, get poor results, and conclude the strategy was wrong. The strategy is not wrong. The prerequisite skills were missing.
The three non-negotiable skills:
👁️ 1. Visual Taste & Design Fundamentals
You need to be able to look at a layout and know when something is off — spacing, hierarchy, contrast, weight. If you cannot tell the difference between a good and bad landing page within 5 seconds, you have a taste gap.
💡
How to close it in 2 weeks
Spend 2 weeks on Mobbin.com and Land-book.com. Study 50 real SaaS landing pages. Build the vocabulary before you build the template.
✍️ 2. Copywriting Fundamentals
Your listing, your X posts, your documentation, your email sequence — all of it is copy. If you write features not benefits, vague not specific, passive not active, your conversion rates will be mediocre regardless of design quality.
💡
How to close it
Read Ogilvy on Advertising once. Read the first 10 posts from the top 3 template sellers on X. Notice what they say and how. Write 10 headlines for your template before you design anything.
🧠 3. Basic Product Thinking
Can you articulate exactly who your buyer is, what problem they have, and why your solution solves it better? If your answer is "designers, they need a nice template," you have a product thinking gap.
✦ Fill this sentence before opening Framer
"My template is for [specific person] who is trying to [specific outcome] but is currently struggling with [specific obstacle]. My template solves this by [specific mechanism]."
If you cannot complete this with specifics → you are not ready to build yet.
Who should NOT follow this playbook yet:
🚫
Not Yet
Never designed a complete site from scratch · Can't articulate why a competitor's template works or doesn't · Never written content with genuine engagement from strangers · Expect revenue in first 2 weeks without an existing audience
✅
This Is Built For You
You've shipped something before · You have a genuine point of view on design · You're willing to post publicly before it's finished · You think in terms of buyer outcomes, not personal preferences

Part 2

Designing & Building the Template

The 3–4 week build plan and technical standards that make or break reviews

👁️
The 5-Second Test
A founder looks at your template and immediately thinks "this looks like a real product." No lorem ipsum, no stock photos of handshakes, no 12-section bloat. Every section exists because it converts — not because other templates have it.
Exact sections to include — and why:
#SectionConversion Purpose
1Hero — "[Product] for [persona] who want [outcome]"Immediately signals this is for them. Creates desire in 5 seconds.
2Social proof bar (logos or short testimonials)Reduces risk. Borrowed credibility before they scroll.
3Feature breakdown (3 columns, not 6)Communicates value without overwhelming. Forces prioritization.
4How it works (3-step visual process)Reduces perceived complexity. Makes the product feel achievable.
5Pricing (2–3 tiers, one highlighted)Anchors value. Middle option does the heavy lifting.
6FAQHandles objections passively. Reduces support load.
7Final CTA / waitlist captureLast conversion opportunity before they leave.
Technical standards — skip any of these and you will receive bad reviews:
Full dark/light mode toggle
Non-negotiable. Build color system around CSS variables from day one.
Mobile-first responsive
Test at 320px, 375px, 390px, 768px, 1280px, and 1440px. All six. Every section.
CMS-ready components
Pricing tables and testimonials hooked into Framer CMS — buyers update content without touching design.
Single-variable color system
One primary color token that cascades everywhere. Buyers rebrand in 60 seconds.
Tasteful Framer Motion animations
Entrance animations on scroll for feature sections, subtle hover states. Nothing like a 2018 Webflow template.
SVG/Lottie illustrations
No stock photos. Curated free-to-use SVG or Lottie assets matching the AI aesthetic.
Kitchen Sink page
A separate page showing every component variant. Makes previews impressive and customization obvious.
Build order & timeline:
WeekFocus
Week 1Desktop design in Framer. Design system first — type scale, color tokens, spacing. Hero, pricing, and feature sections.
Week 2Complete all sections. CMS collections for testimonials and FAQ. Responsive testing across all six breakpoints. No section ships without mobile sign-off.
Week 3Polish: micro-animations, hover states, empty states, form error states. Build Kitchen Sink page. Write documentation.
Week 4Record 5-minute Loom walkthrough. Soft launch to X audience at early access price. Collect feedback. Iterate before public launch.
📧
Productize Your Documentation
Don't just write docs — build an onboarding experience: 3–5 email sequence after purchase (Lemon Squeezy handles this) · "Launch in 2 hours" checklist · Upgrade nudges in email #3 mentioning bundle and agency license. This turns a static product into a guided experience. Buyers who feel supported leave reviews. Buyers who feel abandoned ask for refunds.
Part 3

Building Your Template OS (The Defensibility Layer)

The shared architecture that cheap copycats cannot replicate

🏗️
Why This Was Missing from v1
The original plan had no answer to "what happens when a competitor copies your template for $29?" The Template OS is that answer. It takes 4–6 extra hours to build correctly and gives you a defensible architecture that cheap copycats cannot replicate without rebuilding everything from scratch.
What a Template OS includes:
Shared color token system
10 semantic variables: --brand, --surface, --text-primary, etc.
Shared typography scale
5 sizes, consistent line heights and letter spacing
Shared component library
Buttons, cards, badges, nav, footer — all reusable across every template
Shared CMS schema
Testimonials, pricing, FAQ — same structure across templates
Shared animation presets
5–8 entrance animations buyers can apply to any element
Your visual signature — lock in before template 1:
ElementYour Decision to Make
Primary palette2–3 colors max. One brand color, one neutral system, one accent. Define hex values permanently.
Type pairing1 heading font + 1 body font. Stick to both across all templates. Buyers recognize the pairing.
Border radiusPick one: sharp (0–2px), soft (6–8px), or round (12–16px). Never mix in the same template.
Illustration styleAbstract geometric, line-based, or isometric. One style. Sourced from the same library.
Animation signatureYour entrance animation should feel uniquely yours. Subtle fade-up, spring-based pop, or staggered reveal.
🔗
Cross-Template Compatibility Selling Point
From template two onwards, advertise: "All our templates share the same component library and design tokens — combine sections from any template in minutes." This is a genuine purchasing reason that generic template sellers cannot offer.

Part 6

Build in Public on X — The Full Playbook

Including the writing-from-zero methodology missing from every other guide

✍️
Writing From Zero — The Gap This Section Addresses
There is a significant difference between "post 3–5 times per week" and "post content that people actually want to read." That gap is writing fluency. Most build-in-public advice glosses over it because it is uncomfortable to admit how wide it is.
📝How to Develop Voice Before You Need It
1
Read 30 posts from 3 accounts your buyer follows
Not design accounts. Founder accounts. For each high-engagement post, write down: what emotion does this create? Curiosity? Recognition? Validation? Agreement?
2
Write 10 posts you will never publish
Use this structure for each: Observation (something specific you noticed) → Implication (why it matters for your buyer) → Contrast (what most people do instead). Delete any post where you can't complete all three — the insight isn't sharp enough.
3
Find your one honest opinion about design
Example: "Most SaaS templates are built for agencies, not founders — you can see it in every design decision they make." That's a point of view. "I make templates for founders" is not.
4
Lower the bar for your first 20 posts
The goal is not to go viral. It's to establish the habit of public thinking. The only failure is not posting. A post that gets 3 likes still exists, still gets indexed, still gives you data.
💡
The highest-performing format from zero
The specific observation thread. Not "here are 5 things I learned" but "I spent 4 hours on the hero section. Here is the one decision I almost got wrong, why, and what I did instead." Specificity of "4 hours" and "one decision" creates credibility before the insight lands.
📅Pre-Launch Phase (Weeks 1–4): Building Anticipation
Week 1: Validation post · first design decision (screenshot both options) · dark/light toggle working
Week 2–3: Thought leadership thread (3 things every SaaS template gets wrong) · technical deep-dive · native Loom under 90 seconds · A/B poll on pricing page
Week 4: Soft launch to email list at $39 · documentation post · 7-day countdown post (functions as a landing page in tweet format)

Anti-Saturation Framework

When to enter, when to pivot, when to kill — before committing 4 weeks of build time

Three red flags that mean "Do Not Enter":
🚩Red Flag 1: Top 10 listings all have >4.8 rating and >100 reviews
Market is mature, buyers are satisfied, the bar to displace incumbents is extremely high. You're not entering a market — you're trying to steal share from entrenched, optimized products.
💡
What to do
Go one level narrower. Saturation at category level almost never extends to sub-niche level.
🚩Red Flag 2: No visible complaints to exploit
Scan every review in top 10 listings. If you can't find at least 3 recurring complaints — missing features, poor docs, no dark mode — there's no clear gap. Buyers are satisfied.
💡
What to do
Move to a different category. The template category with the most complaints is your highest-opportunity entry point.
🚩Red Flag 3: Visual styles have already converged
If top 10 listings all look similar — same dark hero, same gradient cards, same glassmorphism — the aesthetic has been commoditized.
💡
What to do
Contrarian aesthetic is the only winning move. If everyone is dark, launch light. If everyone uses gradient cards, use flat minimal cards.

Part 13

Competitive Intelligence Playbook

The exact methodology to identify precisely where to beat the competition

The Competitor Teardown — run this for the top 5 templates before writing a single line of your design brief:
1
Buy or preview the top 3
Don't audit from listing screenshots. Get inside the actual product. Pay the $49. You're buying competitive intelligence worth 10x the price. Note everything that frustrates you — that frustration is your product brief.
2
Score each competitor across 6 dimensions (1–5 scale)
Design quality · Customizability · Speed to launch · Documentation quality · Visual freshness · Mobile execution
3
Map the gap
Take the average score for each dimension across all 5 competitors. Any dimension averaging below 3.5 is your entry point. Build to a 5 in that dimension.
🎯
The Differentiation Decision Rule
Choose your 2 highest-opportunity dimensions (where competitors score lowest), make those your public positioning pillars, and build the rest to "good enough" standard.

Example: If competitors score 2.1 on documentation and 2.4 on mobile, your positioning becomes: "The only AI SaaS template with production-quality mobile and a full documentation hub."
How to beat a top listing with 200+ reviews:
1
Don't compete directly
200 reviews has too much social proof momentum to displace head-on. Go one level narrower.
2
Steal their complaints
Sort reviews by lowest rating. Build the template their buyers wish existed.
3
Win on recency
Older top listings often have dated aesthetics. "Built for 2025" is a legitimate positioning if you can back it up.
4
Win on documentation
Almost no top-selling template has genuinely excellent documentation. The most underexploited competitive gap in the entire market.

Introduce Starter Bundle. Email every past buyer with 25% loyalty discount.
X content attracting creators not founders
Posts get likes but no DM inquiries
Shift topics: less "how I built X," more "how founders can do Y."
Distribution too narrow
100% traffic from one channel
Add YouTube or SEO. One channel failure should not crater your revenue.
😢Good Sales but Bad Reviews
Documentation gap
Reviews mention confusion about customization
Rewrite documentation completely. Add Loom walkthrough. This single fix resolves 70% of bad reviews.
Mobile is broken
Reviews mention mobile layout issues
Stop all marketing. Fix every breakpoint before running any more ads or posts. One bad mobile review undoes five good ones.
Overpromised
Reviews say "not what I expected"
Audit listing copy against actual product. Remove any claims the product doesn't clearly fulfill.
Too hard to customize
Reviews say "hard to change colors" or "confusing structure"
Rebuild color system around a single CSS variable. Add a "Quick Start" page showing the 5 things buyers change first.
😰"Great Product, No Audience" — the Most Common Failure Mode
⚠️
The root cause
You treated the product as the hard part and distribution as the easy part. It's the other way around.
1

Submit to directories

Part 16

Where Judgment Matters More Than the Playbook

Mechanical execution gets you to $1K–$3K/month. Judgment gets you the rest.

🧭
The Honest Framing
This document can systematize the what and the when. It cannot systematize taste. And taste is what separates people who follow this playbook to $500/month from those who follow it to $10K/month.
The four judgment calls the playbook cannot make for you:
🎯Picking the right nuance within a niche
The Market Entry Scoring Model tells you if a niche is enterable. It cannot tell you which specific angle will resonate. "Minimalist AI tools for developer-founders who hate flashy design" is a nuance — and the nuance is where real positioning lives.
💡
How to develop it
Spend 2 hours on X finding founders who launched AI tools. Screenshot the 10 products that feel like they belong together aesthetically. Articulate in one sentence what they share. That sentence is your nuance.
👁️Knowing when design feels "off"
No checklist catches bad design. You either feel it or you don't. Competent sells. Compelling converts at 3x the rate.
💡
How to develop it
Study 100 landing pages from producthunt.com over two weeks. For each one: what is the one thing that makes this feel right or wrong? After 100 pages, your eye will be faster than any checklist.
✍️Writing copy that actually resonates
"Finally, a template that does not make you feel like a designer" implies the buyer's frustration, not just their goal. That implication comes from having listened to enough founders to know what they actually feel.
💡
How to develop it
Before writing a single word of your listing copy, write down 10 complaints a solo founder has about building their landing page. Not goals — complaints. Write your copy as a response to those complaints.
📡Reading market signals that are not data
Knowing which gut instinct to trust and which to override with data only improves with reps.
💡
How to develop it
Write a short retrospective after every significant decision: every post that did well, every post that flopped, every sale, every refund. Over time you'll build a personal model that no generic playbook can replicate.
The things this playbook cannot teach you:
🎨
Pattern recognition from exposure
The more good design you've consumed, the faster you can produce it. It accumulates over months of deliberate attention.
✂️
Editing instinct
Knowing what to remove is harder than knowing what to add. The best templates are not the most complete — they're the most ruthlessly edited.
🗣️
Authentic voice in content
Readers can tell the difference between a real person thinking out loud and a creator following a content formula. The formula gets likes. The voice gets buyers.
⏳
Tolerance for the period before it works
Every template business has a 4–8 week period where you're doing everything right and the numbers aren't moving. Many people stop here. The ones who don't are who Part 8 is written for.
Part 17

Speed vs Quality — The Shipping Decision Framework

Perfectionism masquerading as quality standards is the most common execution failure

⚖️
The Core Rule
Ship faster when distribution is the bottleneck. Polish longer when product quality is the bottleneck. Most first-time template builders have a distribution bottleneck, not a quality bottleneck — which means their default should be "ship faster."
⚡
Ship at 80% when...
You have no existing audience · You're in a fast-moving category · Your competitive gap is positioning, not execution · It's your first template (the gap between what you think the market wants and what it buys is enormous on your first product)

What "80%" means: All sections present and functional. Mobile tested. Documentation covers core 3 use cases. Dark/light works. Kitchen Sink page exists. Animations present but not perfected.
💎
Push to 95% when...
You're entering a mature category with high-quality incumbents · You have an existing audience waiting · Your differentiation claim is design quality · You're building template 3 or 4

What "95%" means: Every section refined. Tested at all 6 breakpoints. Comprehensive documentation with Loom. 3 people outside your circle used it without asking you a single question.
🚫
The One Rule That Overrides Everything
Never ship with broken mobile. Everything else is negotiable. A template with imperfect animations and great mobile converts. A template with perfect animations and broken mobile generates refund requests and 2-star reviews that take months to bury. Mobile is the non-negotiable 100% gate.
Part 18

How to Become the Default Choice in a Micro-Niche

The difference between being one of the good options and being the obvious first choice

🎯
The Target State
When a solo founder types "Framer template" into any search bar, your name or product should be the first thing they find, the first thing their peers recommend, and the first thing they think of. Achievable in a micro-niche with 6–12 months of deliberate positioning.
🥇Early Positioning Dominance
Name your positioning explicitly and publicly
"I build Framer templates for solo founders shipping AI products" — in your X bio, listing tagline, and website headline. State the claim before a competitor does.
Create the category if it doesn't exist
If no one is talking about "AI founder templates" as a distinct category, you can name it, define it, and become its default representative.
Publish content that owns the topic
Write the definitive thread on "what makes an AI SaaS landing page convert." Become the person who produces the reference content in your niche, not just the product.
🎯Audience Density, Not Just Volume
500 followers who are all solo founders building AI tools is more commercially valuable than 5,000 followers who are a general mix.
Follow and engage with your exact buyer persona first
Find every account that posted about launching an AI tool in the last 30 days. Follow, reply genuinely, DM the ones clearly in your niche.
Create content that filters for your buyer
"How I designed the pricing section for a founder with no design budget" attracts founders. "Building in public" attracts other builders. Grow slower with higher buyer density.
Reject the temptation to broaden
Broadening your position to capture more buyers usually captures fewer. You lose the specificity that made your early buyers choose you.
🏅Niche Authority Signals That Compound
"Featured in" or "Used by" logos
5 recognizable logos from products your buyer persona has heard of do more than 100 anonymous testimonials.
Cited by peers
When other creators reference your template or content, that citation is a stronger signal than anything you can say about yourself.
Milestone transparency
"100 templates sold," "first $10K month" — signals real people are buying and creates self-reinforcing perception of authority.
Part 19

Ceiling Awareness — What Happens After $10K/Month

The decisions you make at $5K/month determine whether $10K/month is a ceiling or a floor

The four ceiling triggers — when two or more appear simultaneously, act proactively:
📉
Trigger 1: Flat growth for 3 consecutive months
Flat with consistent content output and no major competitor entry means you've captured most accessible demand in your current niche.
⚔️
Trigger 2: Competitors iterating faster
New templates in your category launching with higher quality at lower prices. You're no longer the default — you're the incumbent being disrupted.
💬
Trigger 3: Best customers asking for what you don't offer
When buyers consistently ask about setup services, custom modifications, or consulting — that's your product roadmap, not noise.
📊
Trigger 4: Content engagement plateaued despite consistent posting
Your audience has learned your patterns and novelty has worn off. Distribution ceiling, not product ceiling. Requires content evolution, not more content.
The four evolution paths — choose based on where buyers are pulling you:
Path 1
🏗️ Template Specialist
Instead of expanding to new niches, go further into your existing one. Build the complete AI founder stack: landing page, changelog, investor deck, onboarding flow. Become the single system a specific buyer type uses from zero to funded.
Best for: Creators who love the product work and have strong niche positioning.
Path 2
🎨 Template + Agency Hybrid
Your template business is your best lead generation tool for a design service. A template buyer converting to a $500–$2,000 setup service is your highest-value customer by far.
Best for: Creators with strong client communication skills who want to grow without more products.
Path 3
📚 Template + Course Creator
Everything you know about building templates, positioning, and growing on X is teachable. A course on "how to build and sell Framer templates" is a natural extension of your brand authority.
Best for: Creators who enjoy teaching and have built a strong personal brand through build-in-public content.
Path 4
🚀 Template → SaaS
Your template business gives you three things most SaaS founders pay years to acquire: a specific niche, an audience in that niche, and deep knowledge of what they need. A SaaS product is a warm launch into a validated market.
Best for: Creators interested in building software and wanting to use the template business as a launchpad.
⏱️
The Transition Timing Rule
The right time to begin exploring your next evolution is when you are at 70–80% of your current ceiling — while you still have momentum, cash flow, and audience attention to direct toward something new. Starting a course at $2K/month and growing is a distraction. Starting at $8K/month and plateauing is strategic.
